Art Investment Glossary

Tap a term to read its definition. Use the controls to expand or collapse everything.

A

Art that does not attempt to represent reality, instead using shapes, colours, and forms for expression.

Archival-quality paper used in prints or storage to prevent yellowing and deterioration.

A fast-drying paint made of pigment suspended in acrylic polymer emulsion, widely used by contemporary artists.

A professional valuation of an artwork’s fair market value for investment, insurance, or resale purposes.

Institutions like Christie’s, Sotheby’s, or Phillips that sell artworks via public bidding, often influencing global market trends.

B

Large-scale international art exhibition, often shaping critical reputation and market demand for participating artists.

Works by established, historically significant artists whose markets are considered more stable.

When an artwork fails to sell at auction because it did not meet the reserve price.

C

A fabric, usually cotton or linen, stretched over a frame and primed as a surface for painting.

A definitive, scholarly listing of all known works by an artist, essential for authentication.

A fee charged by galleries, dealers, or auction houses for facilitating a sale.

A document detailing the physical state of an artwork, vital for valuation and due diligence.

Art created from the mid 20th century to the present, often speculative and more volatile in market value.

D

An art professional or gallery owner who buys and sells works privately.

Two or three paneled artworks designed to be viewed together.

Including art in an investment portfolio to reduce overall risk and volatility.

A printmaking technique where lines are incised into a plate with a sharp needle, producing soft, rich lines.

E

A limited run of prints, sculptures, or photographs made from the same source. Scarcity enhances collectability.

An artist gaining recognition but not yet widely traded on the secondary market.

Painting technique using heated beeswax mixed with pigments. Durable with a distinct surface quality.

Traditional paintings made for display rather than murals or installations.

F

The price an artwork would achieve in an open market transaction between a willing buyer and seller.

An imitation presented as genuine. A core risk in art investing mitigated by provenance and expert vetting.

Technique of painting onto wet plaster. Historically significant but less common as a tradable asset today.

G

When an artist is represented by a gallery that manages exhibitions and sales, often strengthening market value.

High quality inkjet print reproductions. Typically decorative rather than investment grade.

An opaque water based paint often used for studies and smaller works.

I

The difficulty of converting art into cash quickly without losing value.

A market performance benchmark, such as the Mei Moses Index, tracking art as an asset class over time.

Large scale works designed for specific spaces. Significant culturally but often less liquid as investments.

The cost of replacing an artwork if lost or damaged. Typically higher than resale value.

L

A finite run of works, usually numbered and signed by the artist. Scarcity can support value.

Printmaking process using a stone or metal plate. Widely used by artists such as Picasso and Matisse.

A sale that turns an artwork into cash, via auction or private transaction.

M

A work considered among an artist’s best or most representative. Often achieves premium pricing.

The materials used to create an artwork, for example oil, acrylic, bronze, photography.

Artwork that combines multiple materials or techniques in a single piece.

Works produced roughly between the 1860s and 1970s, distinct from contemporary art.

A one off print created from a unique plate. Valued for rarity and painterly qualities.

O

Pigments bound with linseed or other oils. Prized for richness, durability, and depth of colour.

Artists working in Europe before 1800. Historically stable segment of the market.

A one of a kind artwork, not a reproduction or print.

P

Sticks of pigment and binder used for drawings. Fragile but often collectible.

The colouring material in paint or ink, influencing visual quality and longevity.

A collection of works by one artist or held by one investor or institution.

The first sale of an artwork, usually from the artist’s studio or representing gallery.

The documented history of ownership. Critical for verifying authenticity and value.

R

Minimum price set for an auction sale. If not met, the work may remain unsold.

Professional conservation to repair or stabilise artworks. Can affect value positively or negatively.

Financial gain or loss on an artwork relative to its cost, usually expressed as a percentage.

S

Three dimensional artwork created from materials such as stone, bronze, wood, or resin.

The resale market for artworks, primarily auctions and private dealers.

Investment model allowing fractional ownership of artworks through shares or units.

Printmaking technique using stencils and mesh. Popularised in Pop Art by artists such as Warhol.

Artistic genre depicting inanimate subjects. Traditional and widely collected.

T

A physical asset like art that can function as a hedge against inflation and currency risk.

Fast drying paint using egg yolk and pigment. Common in Renaissance panel painting.

Legal ownership rights to an artwork.

U

Refers to artists under 40 or recently emerging on the global market. High growth but more speculative.

A one off work with no duplicates. Generally commands higher value than editioned works.

V

Professional assessment of an artwork’s worth considering artist reputation, provenance, condition, and demand.

A private preview or opening event of an exhibition, often attended by collectors and investors.

W

Informal term for the visual impact of a work on display, which can influence desirability and price.

Paint made with pigment and a water soluble binder. Valued for transparency and delicacy.

Art’s role as a store of value in uncertain economic conditions.
